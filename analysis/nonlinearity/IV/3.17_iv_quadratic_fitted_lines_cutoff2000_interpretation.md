# Interpretation of Updated Quadratic IV Fitted-Line Plot

The updated fitted-line plot [iv_quadratic_fitted_lines_cutoff2000_global.svg](/Users/xiang/github/Index_Fund/analysis/nonlinearity/IV/iv_quadratic_fitted_lines_cutoff2000_global.svg) shows the causal quadratic IV component for each market-quality outcome under the preferred global Russell cutoff `2000` design. Each panel traces the fitted contribution of indexing, `b1 x + b2 x^2`, over the supported range of `ind_own`, with the shaded region marking the empirical support used for interpretation and the vertical marker showing the estimated turning point when it lies inside that range.

For `volatility`, the curve is clearly U-shaped, which is the strongest nonlinear result in the figure. This implies that higher indexing initially reduces volatility, but beyond an interior threshold the marginal benefit weakens and eventually reverses. The turning point is inside the observed support, so this is the cleanest evidence that indexing is beneficial for stability at low-to-moderate levels but not monotonically so.

For `amihud_illiq`, the fitted line suggests a nonlinear pattern as well, but the shape is weaker and should be interpreted more cautiously. The plot indicates that indexing may improve liquidity over part of the support, yet the evidence is less visually decisive than for volatility. This is consistent with the regression output, where the nonlinear effect exists but is not as robustly informative as the volatility channel.

For `price_info`, the fitted curve does not show a similarly compelling or stable interior pattern. The plot suggests that the causal relationship between indexing and price informativeness is weaker and less precisely pinned down in this quadratic IV specification. As a result, the strongest economic takeaway from the figure is not about informativeness, but about volatility.

Overall, the updated plot supports an interior-optimum interpretation of indexing under the IV design. The main causal evidence is that moderate levels of indexing appear beneficial for market stability, while the effects on liquidity are weaker and the effects on price informativeness are not robust. In that sense, the figure reinforces the argument that some indexing is good for the market, but more indexing is not unambiguously better across all dimensions of market quality.
